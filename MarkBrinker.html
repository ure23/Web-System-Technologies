Basic Anatomy Of A Website

Let's start by defining the various parts of a website:

Header

The header is the top section of a website

Years ago, headers often featured big banners

Pre-Header

The pre-header is the thin strip that sits above the main header.

Favicon

A favicon is the small icon that appears in a browser tab

Navigation (aka Navigation Bar or Main Menu)

The navigation is the set of links at the top of a website

On mobile devices, navigation links often transform into an icon

For more complex websites with tons of pages

Breadcrumbs

Breadcrumbs are a type of navigation that shows visitors their location

Home > Blog > Latest Posts > Post Title

Breadcrumbs make it easy for visitors to understand where they are on your site

Website Content

When it comes to written text, you'll often hear it called web copy or body copy

Website content is everything on your site that communicates your message to visitors.

Homepage (or home page)

The homepage — also called the front page or main page

The homepage sets the tone for the entire website.

Common elements on a home page include:

  • A hero area (headline and image) to grab attention.
  • A navigation menu to help visitors find key pages

    • A common website design best practice is to link your logo to the home page.

    Homepage Hero Area (aka Feature Area aka Headline Area)

    The Homepage Hero Area is the top section of a homepage that grabs your attention.

    It often spans the full width of the page and typically includes key elements like:

  • A headline that clearly communicates the site's purpose or the problem it solves.
  • A sub-headline that adds supporting details or context.

    • The term “hero” comes from traditional print media.

    Slider

    A slider is a feature on a website that displays a rotating series of images, like a slideshow.

    Most often find sliders on homepages, either as a replacement for a hero image.

    While sliders used to be a common design trend, their popularity has decreased in recent years.

    Website Content

    When it comes to written text, it's often hear as a web copy or body copy, but website content goes beyond just words.

    Website content is everything on your site that communicates your message to visitors.

    Alt Text

    Alt text (short for “alternative text”) is a brief description added to images on a website.

    It serves two main purposes:

      It makes websites more accessible by describing images to visually impaired users who rely on screen readers.
      It helps search engines understand what an image is about.

    For example, if you have an image of an office space, instead of leaving it without a description.

    Sidebar

    A sidebar is the narrow vertical column that appears alongside your main website content.

    In recent years, many websites have moved away from using sidebars altogether.

    Sub-Footer

    The sub-footer is the section that appears at the bottom of a webpage.

    While its content varies by site, a sub-footer typically includes elements such as:

  • Quick Contact Information: Displaying addresses or phone numbers for immediate reference.
  • Supplementary Navigation Links: Offering shortcuts to key pages like “About Us.”

    • The sub-footer is designed to serve as an added layer of user convenience

    Footer (The "True" Footer)

    The footer is the final, definitive section at the very bottom of a webpage.

    Common footer contents include:

  • Copyright and Legal Disclaimers: Noting trademark rights and the copyright year.
  • Policy Links: Links to the privacy policy, terms of use, and any other legal information.

    • This section is static across the entire site and represents the definitive end point

    Note: Although many people refer to both areas (i.e. sub-footer and footer) as the “footer,” they're actually two different things.

    Landing Page

    A landing page is a specific page on your website where visitors “land” after clicking on an ad.

    The main goal of a landing page is to get the visitor to take a specific action.

    Here's a landing page example.

    Call To Action (aka CTA)

    A call to action (or CTA) is a specific and direct request asking visitors to take a desired action.

    A good call to action doesn't have to be pushy or over-the-top.

    Above the Fold

    “Above the fold” refers to the part of a webpage you immediately see before you start scrolling.

    The term “above the fold” comes from the newspaper industry.

    Blog

    A blog is a type of website—or a section of a larger website—that features content.

    It comes down to how the content is structured and presented:

  • Blog posts are organized chronologically.
  • Web pages (like the homepage, about page, or contact page) are static.

    • Blogs also tend to have a more casual, conversational tone compared to standard web pages.

    For more info on the similarities and differences between a blog and a website,check out my video

    Card Design (aka Tile Design or Grid-Based Design)

    Card design organizes content into visually distinct “cards” or tiles, often arranged in a grid layout.

    Card design can be used in a variety of ways:

  • Image galleries
  • Blog pages
  • Sales pages

    WordPress

    WordPress powers millions of websites worldwide and is one of the most popular tools

    Think of WordPress as the operating system for your website.

    Here's how WordPress works:

  • Themes: A WordPress theme controls how your site looks.
  • Plugins: Plugins add functionality to your site, much like apps on your phone.

    • WordPress Page vs. WordPress Post

    This takes a bit of explaining, so I wrote a whole piece on the similarities.

    Hosting

    To have a website on the internet, it needs to be hosted on a web server.

    Caching

    Caching is the process of storing a temporary version of a website or webpage to help it load faster.

    Caching improves site speed, reduces the load on your web server.

    Domain Name (aka URL)

    A domain name is the address people type into their browser to visit a website—like Amazon.com, CNN.com, or markbrinker.com.

    Every website is identified by an IP address, which is a long string of numbers like 143.398.884.342.

    Domain Registrar

    A domain registrar is where you purchase and register your domain name.

    301 Redirect

    A 301 redirect is a way to permanently send visitors (and search engines) from one URL to another.

    404 Error Page

    A 404 error page appears when someone tries to visit a page on your website that doesn't exist.

    DNS

    DNS stands for Domain Name System.

    As mentioned earlier, humans use domain names (like markbrinker.com) instead of long, hard-to-remember IP addresses.

    HTML

    HTML stands for Hyper Text Markup Language, and it's the standard language used to create websites and web pages.

    Fun fact: HTML inspired the name of the original Hotmail email service, launched in 1996.

    CSS

    CSS stands for Cascading Style Sheets. While HTML is used to create the structure of a webpage, CSS is what makes it look good.

    Website Builder

    A website builder is an all-in-one tool that allows you to design and build a website without needing to write code.

    Popular examples of website builders include Wix and Squarespace.

    Mobile Responsive

    A mobile responsive website automatically morphs to fit the shape and size of the screen.

    More than 50%of all web traffic comes from mobile devices

    SSL

    SSL stands for Secure Socket Layer.

    You can tell if a website is secure by looking for the padlock icon in your browser's address bar:

  • A non-secure website starts with “http” (Hyper Text Transfer Protocol).
  • A secure website starts with “https”, where the “s” stands for secure.

    To get ranked in the organic search listings, you need to produce content that:

  • Is high quality and genuinely helpful.

    • Google Analytics

    Google Analyticsis a free service from Google that provides statistics about your website.

    By collecting this data, Google Analytics removes the guesswork.

    Email Service Provider (ESP)

    An email service provider (sometimes called an email management service or EMS) helps you build, manage, and communicate.

    Using an email service provider offers several key benefits:

  • Automation
  • Scalability
  • Deliverability
  • Compliance
  • Reporting & Tracking

    • An email service provider makes email marketing more efficient, reliable, and measurable.

    Conclusion

    I hope you found this mini crash course on website terminology helpful.

    The good news? You don't need to understand all the tech behind the scenes.
